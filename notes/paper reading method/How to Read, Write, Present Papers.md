# How to Read, Write, Present Papers

Based on the presentation by **Nitin H. Vaidya** (University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, 2002)[cite: 5].

---

## 1. General Caveats & Summary
*   **Statutory Warning:** These are opinions, and your advisor may not agree with all of them[cite: 5].
*   **The Golden Rules:** Use common sense, learn from experience, and "do unto others as you would have them do unto you"[cite: 5].

## 2. How to Read a Paper
*   **Selective Reading:** You cannot and should not read everything[cite: 5]. Reading too many solutions using a particular theme can suppress innovation and make it hard to think differently[cite: 5].
*   **What to Read:** Focus on the Abstract, Introduction, Motivation, Problem description, Solution, Performance Analysis, Conclusions, and Future Work[cite: 5].
*   **What to Note:** 
    *   Authors and their research group (know where to look for future papers on this topic)[cite: 5].
    *   The core theme of the solution and approach to performance evaluation[cite: 5].
    *   Any shortcomings in the paper[cite: 5].
*   **Be Skeptical:** Just because a paper is in print doesn't mean it is flawless[cite: 5]. If it sounds too good to be true, it often is[cite: 5].

## 3. How to Write a Paper
*   **Prioritize Clarity:** Good writing makes a significant difference[cite: 5]. Most papers are not exceptional, so readability is a must[cite: 5]. It is better to say a little clearly than to say too much unclearly[cite: 5].
*   **Do Not Irritate the Reader:**
    *   Define all notation before use and summarize it in one place; no one is impressed by Greek symbols anymore[cite: 5].
    *   Avoid Using Too Many Acronyms (AUTMA)[cite: 5]. Do not assume the reader knows or cares about them[cite: 5].
*   **Writing Theory Papers:** Unreadability is not the same as formalism[cite: 5]. Readers should understand contributions without reading every detail[cite: 5]. Relegate unimportant proofs to an appendix; proof techniques are often more valuable than the proofs themselves[cite: 5].
*   **Writing Systems Papers:**
    *   Explain how your work relates to the state of the art and discuss relevant past work (those authors might be reviewing your paper)[cite: 5].
    *   **Be Polite:** Avoid offending people[cite: 5]. Instead of saying "The scheme presented by X performs terribly," say "The scheme by X does not perform as well in scenario Y"[cite: 5].
*   **Embrace Weaknesses:** Tell the reader your shortcomings[cite: 5]. People appreciate a balanced presentation if your ideas do not work well in certain scenarios[cite: 5].
*   **Technical Reports:** Useful for getting early feedback, timestamping your work, and including extra results that don't fit in the main paper[cite: 5].

## 4. How to Present a Paper
*   **Primary Objectives:** Keep the audience awake and attentive (humor helps), and clearly get the problem definition across (audience members might not work in your specific problem area)[cite: 5].
*   **Talk Outline:** Skip for short talks; include and make it interesting for long talks[cite: 5].
*   **Slide Design:**
    *   Slide text does not need to be grammatically accurate; keep it short and omit minor details (they can read the paper for exact algorithms)[cite: 5].
    *   Avoid fancy backgrounds and gratuitous animations; standard layouts with decent font sizes and colors are best[cite: 5].
    *   "A picture is worth 1000 words"—use illustrations to explain complex algorithms[cite: 5].
*   **Time Management & Pacing:**
    *   Rule of thumb: 1 slide for every 1-2 minutes[cite: 5].
    *   For short talks, focus on explaining one or two ideas well rather than explaining many ideas poorly[cite: 5].
    *   Anticipate technical questions and prepare backup explanatory slides[cite: 5].
*   **Delivery:**
    *   Point to the screen, not the slide on the projector, and avoid blocking the screen[cite: 5].
    *   Talk slowly, especially if English is your second language or you have an accent[cite: 5]. 
    *   Practice makes perfect (or at least tolerable), especially for pacing and fitting the time limit[cite: 5].
