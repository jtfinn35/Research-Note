# How to Look for Ideas in Computer Science Research

---

## 1. The Challenge of Finding Ideas
Coming up with a good research idea is often the most difficult step of a research project[cite: 7]. 
*   If your advisor hands you concrete ideas, you get publications quickly but lose the chance to train your independent idea-finding skills[cite: 7].
*   If your advisor only gives high-level directions, it is a make-or-break situation where you must learn to find ideas or risk failing[cite: 7].
*   Therefore, training yourself to look for research ideas as early as possible is critical[cite: 7].

## 2. Tip 1: Read Papers and Develop Your Taste
Ideas do not come out of the blue; they come from reading and getting inspired[cite: 7].
*   You do not have to understand *all* technical details to consider a paper read[cite: 7]. The goal is to appreciate and criticize the ideas (e.g., "What makes an interesting paper?")[cite: 7].
*   Find out what types of papers interest you most and ask yourself *why*[cite: 7]. Do you like novel attacks? System defenses? Formal methods? This helps narrow your scope and develop your unique research taste[cite: 7].

## 3. Tip 2: Six Patterns of Developing Research Ideas
Every paper has a story, but their origins usually fall into a few patterns. Mastering these makes finding ideas easier[cite: 7].

1.  **Fill in the blank:** Read broadly on a topic, jot down notes on dimensions (assumptions, methodologies, techniques), and draw a table. The empty spots in the table are potential new ideas (e.g., if static analysis was used, try dynamic analysis)[cite: 7].
2.  **Expansion:** A natural progression of "fill in the blank." If you've already published in a space, look at your strict assumptions or requirements and try to eliminate or relax them to find new pathways[cite: 7].
3.  **Build a hammer and find nails:** Develop a strong, unique expertise, tool, or dataset that others cannot easily replicate (the hammer). Use it to find and solve interesting new problems (the nails)[cite: 7]. *Note: This is impactful but time-consuming to build.*
4.  **Start small, then generalize:** Start with a small, intriguing observation. If the anomaly is deeply rooted, cannot be easily explained by well-known concepts, and is not a one-off event, dig deeper to generalize it into a full paper[cite: 7].
5.  **Reproduction of prior work:** Try to reproduce a published paper. You might find inadvertent mistakes, un-reproducible results, or biased benchmarks. Identifying these discrepancies often reveals room for improvement and new insights[cite: 7].
6.  **External sources:** Look outside academia. Connect with industry people to learn their pain points—they often need reliable solutions, and academic research can provide exploratory answers. Alternatively, get inspired by tech news feeds (e.g., Twitter)[cite: 7].
    *   *Cybersecurity specifics:* Adversarial research (breaking a defense or building a new one) and Automating a manual process (e.g., bug triage)[cite: 7].

## 4. Tip 3: Develop a Good Habit of Thinking
Execution is fundamentally different from idea generation. You must consciously practice finding ideas[cite: 7]:
*   **Keep reading periodically:** Do not get completely absorbed by your current project. Scan titles and read abstracts of new conference papers[cite: 7].
*   **Take paper reviews seriously:** Reviewing (especially rejected papers) helps you learn "how the sausage is made" and what falls below the bar[cite: 7].
*   **Be curious and read broadly:** Many good ideas come from the crossovers of different fields[cite: 7].
*   **Attend reading groups:** Ask questions. A good exercise is to debate a paper (attacker vs. defender) to see how defensible an idea is[cite: 7].
*   **Talk to your labmates:** Create a connection, discuss papers casually, and bounce ideas off each other to get fresh perspectives when stuck[cite: 7].
